Welcome to the machine:
more than a song, a reality

By Juan Camilo Veloza Published on October 31, 2023

In Welcome to the Machine, Pink Floyd denounces the dehumanization caused by institutions and the music industry, a theme that still resonates today with the questions raised by technology and the modification of human nature.

The origin of the song

Their album The Dark Side of the Moon (1973) propelled them to rock legend status, with themes exploring madness, capitalism, and the human condition. In 1975, they released Wish You Were Here, an album that reflects their frustration with the music industry, while paying tribute to their former leader, Syd Barrett. Welcome to the Machine is the second song on this album, a scathing critique of society and the industry that crushes the individual to make them an obedient "machine".


Who is Pink Floyd

Pink Floyd is one of the most influential progressive rock bands in the history of music. Formed in 1965 in London, the band originally consisted of Syd Barrett, Roger Waters, Nick Mason, and Richard Wright. Later, David Gilmour joined the band to replace Barrett, whose mental health was declining. They are known for their experimental compositions, innovative concepts, and albums that often denounce the flaws of society.


Analysis of Welcome to the
machine: Between mechanized
society and human alienation

Welcome to the Machine is a central work for understanding Pink Floyd's critique of a modern society that, in their eyes, reduces the individual to a mere cog in a large industrial and commercial machine. This song explores themes of dehumanization, loss of control, and conformity imposed by institutions, particularly the music industry.


The message of the song

The lyrics describe the story of a young man who enters the music world, full of dreams and ambitions, but quickly finds himself trapped by the impersonal and devouring forces of the system. "Welcome my son, Welcome to the Machine", is a phrase that symbolizes this cynical welcome into a world where the individual is shaped by expectations and pre-established norms, with no room for authenticity


A critique of transhumanist
society

This critique of mechanized society echoes the reflections addressed in texts on transhumanism and technology. Similar to the concerns raised by Christian Byk in Transhumanism, where man risks transforming into a "human machine", Pink Floyd's song anticipates a world where the individual loses their human nature by conforming to an oppressive industrial system.


The idea that technology, or in this case the industry, alienates humans by transforming them into machines resonates deeply with contemporary concerns about the modification of human biology and the control of human nature by technological and commercial institutions.

The conclusion of the song

The song, with its dystopian atmosphere, also joins the questioning of the control of technological innovations and their impact on society, as mentioned in discussions on CRISPR technology and transhumanism. Welcome to the Machine expresses this fear that technological advances, if not controlled, will lead to uniformity and a loss of human identity. The same goes for the critique of social inequalities: in this mechanized world, only those who control the machine (or technology) benefit, leaving others alienated or marginalized.


Just like a mirror of these reflections,
the film Gattaca by Andrew Niccol raises crucial questions on the theme of DNA modification, technological advances, and the identity of individuals predetermined by their genetics.
