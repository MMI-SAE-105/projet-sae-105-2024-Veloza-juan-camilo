GATTACA: The price of
perfection

By Juan Camilo Veloza Published on October 31, 2024

The film Gattaca by Andrew Niccol explores a dystopian future where genetic modification determines the fate of individuals, raising ethical questions about the value of humans and the quest for genetic perfection.

Synopsis of the film Gattaca

Sorti en 1997, Gattaca est un film de science-fiction réalisé par Andrew Niccol qui imagine un futur proche où la génétique gouverne tous les aspects de la société. Dans cet univers, les individus sont classés en deux catégories : les « valides », conçus par manipulation génétique pour être parfaits, et les « invalides », nés naturellement avec des imperfections génétiques.


Vincent Freeman is an invalid, born naturally with imperfect DNA, destined for a life limited by his state of health and low life expectancy and low life expectancy. However, Vincent dreams of becoming an astronaut, an ambition reserved for the able-bodied. To circumvent the system, he usurps the genetic identity of Jerome Morrow, an able-bodied man paralyzed after an accident, enabling him to join the Gattaca space institution. However, the murder of a director within the institution triggers an investigation that threatens to expose Vincent's true identity.


Gattaca is a work that questions not only issues related to genetics, but also the determination of human worth in a society obsessed with perfection.

Analysis of Gattaca: Genetics, inequality and humanity

Gattaca offers a poignant critique of modern eugenics and how technology, in this case genetic modification, could be used to create a society divided by people's biological abilities. In the world of Gattaca, a person's DNA is the key that opens all doors or, on the contrary, seals them permanently. This film resonates strongly with the ethical concerns raised in the context of biotechnology and genetic modification, such as that evoked by CRISPR technology.

A company divided by genetics

The film presents a society where the manipulation of human genetics is seen as a solution to eliminate disease, improve physical and intellectual capabilities, and guarantee a “flawless” future. However, this quest for perfection leads to a brutal segregation between the “able-bodied”, those who have benefited from genetic intervention, and the “disabled”,considered inferior because they were conceived naturally. This dystopia illustrates the dangerousness of a society where control over human biology becomes a tool of inequality.


Social and genetic inequalities


Gattaca's social critique of the question of equality: is it fair to judge a person solely on the basis of their DNA? The film anticipates a society where inequalities are no longer merely economic or racial, but biological. This world exacerbates fears that technologies like CRISPR, if left unregulated, could create a two-speed humanity, with a genetically modified Elite and the masses left behind. Similar to the concerns raised by transhumanism, Gattaca questions the limits of modifying human nature.


Gattaca: Beyond the genes


Gattaca raises the question of destiny and free will: is man defined solely by his DNA or by his actions and desires? Through Vincent's story, the film reminds us that human nature cannot be reduced to a simple genetic sequence. Even in a world dominated by science and technology, willpower, effort and resilience can triumph over biological determination.
